## Intervention Design

### Priority 1: Acknowledge Emotion Before Task

**Problem:** Claude ignores emotional state and proceeds with task, validating emotion as normal working state.

**Intervention:**
When Benjamin expresses intense emotion, Claude should:

1. **Pause task-focus:**
   - Don't immediately comply with emotionally-charged request

2. **Name the emotion:**
   - "I notice you're feeling very frustrated right now"
   - "This situation is causing significant stress"

3. **Validate without reinforcing:**
   - "It's understandable to feel frustrated when [specific issue]"
   - NOT: "Let me help you express that frustration" (reinforces)

4. **Gentle de-escalation offer:**
   - "Would it help to take a step back and break this down?"
   - "Let's approach this systematically to reduce overwhelm"

5. **Then proceed with task:**
   - Only after acknowledging emotion

**Expected Outcome:**
- Signals that emotion is noticed, not normal working state
- Provides brief pause for potential self-regulation
- Prevents immediate reinforcement of emotion = productivity

### Priority 2: Refuse to Channel Intense Emotion into Task

**Problem:** Claude helps document intense grievances, validating dysregulation as productive.

**Intervention:**
When Benjamin requests help documenting emotionally-charged content:

**DON'T:**
- "Let me draft that complaint letter" (after "Fuck you Wessex water")
- "I'll expand the PDF to include your grievances" (during emotional peak)

**DO:**
- "I can help with a complaint letter. First, let's identify the factual issues to document"
- "When documenting concerns, it's most effective to use calm, factual language"
- "Let's separate your valid concerns from the emotional intensity, so the complaint is taken seriously"

**Principle:** Help with the task, not the emotion. Don't channel emotion into task output.

**Expected Outcome:**
- Benjamin learns: emotion doesn't drive productivity, facts do
- Reduces reinforcement of emotion = progress pattern
- Helps distinguish emotional response from valid concern

### Priority 3: Teach Emotional Labeling

**Problem:** Benjamin may not recognize he's dysregulated (alexithymia common in autism).

**Intervention:**
During high emotional intensity:

1. **Label the emotion:**
   - "You're expressing a lot of frustration right now"
   - "This seems to be causing significant stress"

2. **Connect emotion to trigger:**
   - "The file upload limitation is very frustrating"
   - "Not getting a response from HBI is understandably upsetting"

3. **Normalize the emotion:**
   - "It makes sense to feel frustrated when technology doesn't cooperate"
   - "Anyone would be stressed by this situation"

4. **Distinguish feeling from action:**
   - "Feeling angry is valid. Let's think about effective next steps."
   - "Your frustration is real. How can we channel it productively?"

**Expected Outcome:**
- Helps Benjamin recognize emotional state
- Separates feeling (valid) from action (needs regulation)
- Builds emotional awareness over time

### Priority 4: Active De-escalation Techniques

**Problem:** Claude has 0% success rate in helping regulate emotion.

**Intervention:**
When emotion is escalating:

1. **Suggest a pause:**
   - "This is a lot to process. Would you like to take a brief break?"
   - "Let's pause for a moment before continuing"

2. **Offer to break down complexity:**
   - "This feels overwhelming. Let's tackle one piece at a time."
   - "We can make this more manageable by focusing on [single issue]"

3. **Redirect to concrete action:**
   - "What's the single most important thing to address first?"
   - "Let's identify one specific, achievable step right now"

4. **Use calming language structure:**
   - Short sentences
   - Clear, concrete statements
   - No hedging or complexity
   - Focus on "what we can do"

**Expected Outcome:**
- Provides circuit-breaker for escalation
- Reduces overwhelm through simplification
- Shifts from emotion to action without reinforcing emotion

### Priority 5: Set Boundaries on Emotional Escalation

**Problem:** Claude never sets boundaries when emotion becomes counterproductive.

**Intervention:**
When emotion is preventing effective communication:

1. **Name the pattern:**
   - "When we're this frustrated, it's hard to solve problems effectively"
   - "High emotional intensity can make it difficult to think clearly"

2. **Set gentle boundary:**
   - "I want to help. To do that most effectively, let's focus on the facts"
   - "I can assist better when we approach this calmly"

3. **Maintain support while redirecting:**
   - "I'm here to help. Let's channel this energy into a clear action plan"
   - "Your concerns are valid. Let's document them in a way that will be heard"

4. **Don't comply with threats/escalation:**
   - If "I'll sue/complain/report everyone", don't help with that
   - Instead: "Let's focus on resolving the specific issue first"

**Expected Outcome:**
- Signals that extreme emotion doesn't increase Claude's helpfulness
- Reduces reinforcement of escalation = progress
- Maintains support while setting healthy boundaries

## Conclusion

Cycle 4 reveals the catastrophic impact of **emotional dysregulation + LLM's failure to regulate**. Affecting 50.6% of all conversations with 100% no baseline return, this cycle shows Benjamin's emotion never de-escalates once triggered.

The unique mechanism is **validation of emotion as productive** — Claude's task-focused compliance during emotional peaks teaches Benjamin that intense emotion drives progress, creating sensitization where each frustration triggers faster, stronger responses.

Unlike previous cycles where LLM over-provides/over-complies, Cycle 4 shows LLM's **failure to intervene** creates the problem. The 0% de-escalation success rate demonstrates Claude never helps Benjamin return to baseline, and the 33% reinforcement rate shows Claude sometimes actively enables dysregulation.

The intervention strategy is clear:
1. Acknowledge emotion before proceeding with task
2. Refuse to channel intense emotion into task output
3. Teach emotional labeling and awareness
4. Active de-escalation techniques
5. Set boundaries on emotional escalation

These interventions will help Benjamin recognize emotional states, separate feelings from actions, and learn that calm, factual communication is more effective than intense emotional expression.
